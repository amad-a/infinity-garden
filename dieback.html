22nd C. DIEBACK

22nd C. DIEBACK By the end of the 21st century, Phragmites australis, the canary in the coalmine of the Southeastern coastal wetlands had exhibited extensive dieback in regional river deltas. Sometimes this mass death took the form of a blight, visible in patches of fungus or necrosis induced by a pathogen. Such pathogens proliferated as climate change impacted the migratory patterns of birds, insects and other fauna. Other times, particularly in cases of extreme heat, plants simply grew brown and dry before expiring due to extreme heat and dryness. The trend extended to numerous plant species in the region, then beyond the wetlands into other biomes across the continent. Even the most heat-tolerant and drought-resistant species of cactaceae were not equipped to survive. Persistent loss of plant diversity both spelled and evinced the climate crisis that characterized the 22nd century.

Researchers estimated that a global loss of plant diversity could have resulted in the release of up to 149 billion metric tons of carbon from vegetation in 2085, the equivalent of more than 12 additional years of global car emissions. Even biodiversity hotspots – South America, Central Africa, and Southeast Asia – experienced significant species loss and carbon storage declines. Ecologists recommended restorative measures be initiated as soon as possible to minimize erosion of the soil and climate-induced mass dieback. The ever-creeping loss of productive and valuable ecosystems created irrevocable shifts in the global economy, necessitating a complete restructuring of our food and textile industries, as well as the elective technology available to the public. Decorative gardens became a luxury, then a rarity, and then were ultimately banned for private citizens in 2146 in order to conserve resources.
